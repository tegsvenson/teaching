Lesson 7: Tools for Prototyping

This lesson provides you with information about what to choose as the tool for prototyping. You are required to make a selection by the time you submit your Project Description. 

 Considerations for Choosing the Prototyping Tool 

Understanding your goal is fundamental to choosing the correct tool for achieving your goal effectively. The goal for your prototype is to have proper interactions that provide a test environment for your idea. The following are the primary goals for your prototype:

  1. Pursue implementing good interaction over graphics. In other words, the goal for the prototype is to come up with a good representation of the functions that you envisioned in your project description rather than having a beautiful or realistic replica of an app (unless beautifying an app is your whole idea).
  2. Keep your ideas and alternative designs as simple as possible. This will make your workload reasonable. I will help you calibrate the complexity of your prototyping goals in the individual meetings. Review prototypes on this page as a reference for an absolute high-fidelity prototype. Coming up with a prototype at this level of fidelity means that you did an absolutely perfect job, overkill for this course's purposes. A much simpler prototype will be more than enough. On the contrary, the lowest visual fidelity example looks like this, which is created using Balsamiq. 
  3. The prototypes should be capable of testing your ideas.

With the goals provided above, your own goals based on your idea for the alternative design, and the reference above, you should be able to decide your tool for your project. Also, see this page for more information about the project assignments. 

 List of Different Apps for Prototyping

The main focus of this course is learning a systematic approach to understanding how technological artifacts can affect our experience. This course does not require you to stick to any specific application for creating your prototype as long as you can create one that properly demonstrates your alternative design and lets you test your idea with users. Learning how to use any of the following tools is not the main focus of this course. The instructor will not consult you on how to use each tool, but there are many resources to learn those skills. 

The following is a list of tools for prototyping, along with my two cents:

Figma

Figma is currently the standard tool for interaction design for professionals. I recommend learning how to use this tool if you are interested in working in the field of UI/UX. This is arguably the best tool for testing most smartphone apps or web pages.
The learning curve for Figma is relatively steep, but it ranges depending on how much you want to achieve with this tool. I personally view the tool's difficulty as similar to Adobe CC apps (Photoshop, Illustrator, Premiere Pro, etc.). Basic functions are relatively easy to learn, but things get more challenging if you want to implement sophisticated functions. You will likely be able to complete the requirements in this course with the free-tier license.

  • Here is Figma.
  • Here is a beginner course for Figma.

Axure

Axure used to be the go-to tool for UI/UX before Figma became prominent. There are many speculated reasons for why that happened, but Axure may seem a bit dated. However, Axure is slightly easier to use for new learners and less taxing for your computer to work on. It should cover most interface designers' needs that Figma covers, albeit less or more convenient in different ways. Students can apply for a free educational license.

  • Here is a beginner tutorial video series from Axure.

Balsamiq

Balsamiq is similar to Axure, but even easier to use. This comes with a downside - Balsamiq is also limited to lower fidelity for its visual representation and the functions that you can implement in your prototype using this tool. This is technically a wireframing tool, rather than a full-scale prototyping tool. Balsamiq is not free to use by default, but I have requested an educational license. We will see if they provide a class-wide invitation for their license. There will be an announcement about access to this tool later this semester.

Powerpoint, Keynote, or Adobe Creative Cloud Apps

Powerpoint, Keynote (Apple's slide creation tool), other web-based presentation slides such as Canva or Prezi can become tools for creating interactive prototypes, or Adobe Creative Cloud apps (such as Illustrator and Photoshop), to create prototypes, especially when using them in tandem. As they are not dedicated prototyping tools, it can be a chore to find the correct assets (for example, icons for certain buttons in an interface) and create sleek interactions for testing. These are easier to start with, but you may later find they make it harder for you to achieve your goals.

Paper Prototyping

Paper prototyping is a crude yet simple and effective way to test your idea with users. You simply draw the interfaces on paper and then present different pages of paper depending on the user's choices. The idea here is similar to Choose Your Own Adventure books or the Bandersnatch episode of Black Mirror from several years ago. This is obviously a less sophisticated option for prototyping. This is more of a tool for wireframes than a prototype. Choosing this option as the only or the primary means to test your prototype will result in receiving a 10% penalty in your grade for all project assignments and the final assignment since other routes require significantly more effort, and you will likely finish this course by learning any skill whatsoever.

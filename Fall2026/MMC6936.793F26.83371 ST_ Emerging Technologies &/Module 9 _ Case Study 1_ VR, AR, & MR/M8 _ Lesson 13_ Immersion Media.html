Lesson 13: Immersive Media 

 

 Lesson

Recap

In our last lesson, we briefly discussed what VR, AR, and MR mean. It is tempting to define these as a set of technologies or devices. However, technological specifications change quickly. Categorizing them into three or more categories with fancy names can be good for marketing when you want to differentiate your product. Still, VR, AR, and MR are all on a continuum, as seen in the figure below.

The Reality-Virtual Reality Continuum

So, the VR/AR/MR/etc. categories may not make much sense. Instead, let's call them together as immersive media. Let's also not get hung up on what immersive media exactly is but rather consider them as a class of content, devices, or platforms that provide an immersive experience. And then, let's define immersive experience using a term called PRESENCE.

We are not really focusing on understanding the technology itself in this course. We are not learning how to design schematics for an electronic device - we are interested in how people use and react to those technologies. In that sense, understanding VR, AR, or MR as a function of user experience, namely presence, is more fitting to our goal.

What is Presence

Most simply put, presence is often defined as "being there" in a virtual environment, compared to being here (i.e., the reality). There are many different approaches and ways to cut the concept. However, most scholars identify three important types of presence based on evolutionary psychology. This lesson's required reading will guide you through the logic, meaning, and different types of presence. When going through them, make sure you check the parts about social presence:

Lee, K. M. (2004). Presence, explicated. Communication Theory, 14(1), 27–50.

Why immersive media?

So, what is up with immersive media? Meta's venture into developing HMDs (Head-Mount Displays) resulted in a $4.4 billion dollar loss in just the third quarter of 2024. Apple's Vision Pro sales are also reportedly lackluster. It could be that they were just following the hype, just like what people did with Bitcoins and NFT. Are both Meta and Apple that delusional? Or was it just some novelty effect, where something new seems automatically more attractive, which wears off over time?

Nintendo failed at VR too.

Maybe, or maybe not. The market saw a boom in HMD VR/AR devices in the past ten years or so. This is not the first time, though. There was another boom in the late 1980s and early 1990s. Nintendo also attempted and failed to sell headsets. HMD devices to create immersive experiences have been around quite longer than you may think, dating back to the 1940s.

The technology became sophisticated throughout the years, but their goal remains almost the same. What are the companies trying to do with this?

Full Circle: Attention and Presence

There are many speculations and analyses about these, but let's focus on two main ideas - attention and presence.

The attention part is quite simple. Earlier this semester, we learned that one of the main goals of the technologies, companies, or any message sender is to capture and maintain the user's attention. Keeping the user's attention is ever so difficult since there are so many content, platforms, and services to use. HMDs solve this issue quickly by creating a physical barrier that stops one from looking away, like a horse's blinker.

HMDs provide a physical barrier to keep user's attention - but that's not all.

That's not all. Social psychologist Robert Cialdini published a lot of research on persuasion. One insight regarding persuasion that he shares in his book is foot-in-the-door, where the salesperson (i.e., the persuader) has to first put their foot in the door of the house that they are visiting. This may feel intrusive, but what it means is that being closer, or having presence, is one of the keys to a successful persuasion.

Likewise, many researches found that increasing social presence in virtual environments increases the probability of changing people's attitudes (e.g., Skalski & Tamborini, 2007). Again, as we discussed earlier, people's attitude change is a precursor for behavioral change. When people grow to like a product (i.e., a positive attitude), even a slight bit, they become more likely to buy the product (behavioral change). Having users wear a 360-vision screen is has been repeatedly shown to elicit the highest presence to the users.

Conclusion

The perks of immersive media are not just about having fun. Being immersive is indeed somewhat related to having more fun, but that does not justify why so much money is spent on developing the seemingly repeatedly failing group of products. The content and the form are significantly more capable of keeping the user's attention, both physically and mentally.

Prototyping Project Assignment Overview

This page introduces you to how the whole prototyping project works. As the prototype project also affects your final project as well, this page essentially explains how to approach the major part of your work in this course this semester.

Note. Some of the links on this page may not work for you until their modules are open. This is intended. You can email me (song100@usf.edu) if you have specific questions about more information regarding the assignments. 

 Prototyping Project Assignment Descriptions

The Prototyping Project is divided into different pieces of assignments. This is to help you focus on one aspect of the whole process at a time. These individual assignments are numbered and meant to be approached sequentially. Students are expected to first finish the earlier assignment before starting the next one.

1. Proposal

You come up with a proposal, which some of you might have finished, and others might still be working on. This is literally a proposal, meaning your idea need not be perfect. You will have a chance to fix things from the proposal. Still, you are expected to share your systematic reasoning for your idea by applying theories that you have learned so far in this course.

2. Individual Meeting 1

You and I have individual meetings. The purpose of this meeting is for you and I to discuss your idea from your proposal, check its feasibility, and add or remove ideas if necessary. You do not have to prepare much for this meeting except for any questions you might have for me. The meeting will be recorded and transcribed, so you can get back to it later if you need to.

3. Identifying the Problem Space

You identify the problem space by interviewing others. You will interview and be interviewed by your classmate. Your own idea will likely sound very compelling to you. That does not mean you have a great idea, even if I found your idea interesting. Therefore, you are conducting an interview with a classmate and hearing their third-party (i.e., user) opinion about your idea.

4. Project Description

You come up with a finalized project description by combining your own idea that is backed up by your experience and theories, your instructor's thoughts about your ideas, and how a random user thinks about your idea. By successfully completing this assignment, you will also receive all the points that you might have lost in your proposal as an extra credit. This will likely be an updated version of your proposal but incorporates the information that you found by sharing your idea with others.

5. Early Prototype

You create an early prototype. This will likely be a wireframe, a mockup, or even just a few drawings. This is a soft deadline for your test-ready prototype, as there are zero points designated for this assignment.

6. Test-Ready Prototype

You create a test-ready prototype. What it means to be test-ready depends on your individual project and the goal you have for your interaction design. Similar to the proposal, your prototype does not have to be perfect. You will have a chance to recuperate.

8. Peer Review the Prototypes

You conduct a peer review with your classmates. Create a single-paragraph description of your project and share it along with your prototype on the class-wide discussion board. You select one of your classmate's projects and provide a review for them.

9. Test the Prototypes

Test the prototype with users. Earlier in the semester, you learned how to quantify user experience. You replicate the method that you learned in the earlier assignment on your prototype with 3 users.

10. Final Report

Create a final report. You incorporate your findings from the quantitative test and your peer's qualitative review into a nicely written report and come to a conclusion and future direction. This is your final paper in this course. You will recuperate points that you might have lost in your prototype assignment by satisfying the additional requirements from the second individual meeting.

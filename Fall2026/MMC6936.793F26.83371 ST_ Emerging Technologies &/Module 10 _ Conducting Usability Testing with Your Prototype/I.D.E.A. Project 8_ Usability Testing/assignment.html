IDEA 8 | Usability Testing

We have tried establishing evidence for usability in an assignment earlier this semester. We are applying that tool to your design alternative. 

A Mac or Windows computer is required to complete this assignment. This course assumes and requires that students use a Mac or Windows computer as their main device for accessing course content. If you have limited access to a personal computer, libraries at USF provide laptop loaning: https://lib.usf.edu/services/laptop-checkout

In this assignment, we practice a systematic approach to measuring user experience. It is easy to claim that an app is user-friendly or not. These claims only become meaningful with adequate support. We are creating supporting evidence in this exercise. 

Checklist before starting usability testing

The following are the ingredients that should be ready before you start testing:

  • Prototype that you created, ready for testing.
  • Existing solution, which is an comparable app/service/functions that are out there already, which you will compare against yours in terms of usability.
  • Three individuals who will be the participants will test your prototype. Meeting in person will make things easier for you.
  • A step-by-step guide on how to complete the tasks using the prototype and the comparison interface.
  • Audio and video recording devices, either on your computer or external ones (usually your phone or tablet), are ready to capture user behavior. Having a Mac or Windows computer is a requirement for this course. If you need one, here is one resource:  https://lib.usf.edu/services/laptop-checkout
  • Questionnaire that you will ask your participants in the interview. See "Data of Interest" for more information.

Testing Procedures

You will prove that your idea from the proposal, which became your project description, that materialized as your prototype, is better than the existing solution. To do so, you will conduct a very basic AB testing. That means you will ask people to use both your prototype and the existing solution, then compare the experiences. You will do so by collecting user behavior indicators and conducting a follow-up interview with the user. The following is what will happen:

  1. Meet the participant
    • I recommend finding a quiet and private place so that the test procedure is not interrupted.
    • You will have three participants in total; meet only one person at a time, and ask the participants not to share the information with each other about your project until both of them finish their participation.
  2. Start recording audio for your conversation with your participant. The audio recording should cover the entire session, from start to finish.
  3. Run screen recording on the device while the user will use to test the usability of your prototype.
    • How to record screen with mouse clicks on 
      • macOS: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=75q-gO2oiC0
      • Windows: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PJB7pM5bvNI&t=10s
    • Make sure you collect mouse clicks (or touching screens) and keyboard inputs by default
  4. Have your user go through their task while you are recording the screen.
  5. Your participant finishes their task on both your prototype and the existing solution.
  6. Conduct the interview with the participant to collect the Data of Interest by conducting a brief interview/questionnaire with the questions below.
  7. A single session of usability testing has been completed. You can stop the audio recording and dismiss your participant.
  8. Repeat steps 1 to 7 with your next and last participant.
  9. Draft an analysis report based on the findings. This part is reserved for the next assignment, IDEA 8. I recommend reviewing IDEA 8 before you work on this one, making sure that you understand what information you will report.

Data of Interest

You collect the data of interest immediately after users complete the tasks you provide.

Collect the following data to test the Effectiveness of your prototype vs. the existing solution:

  • Fewer inputs (mouse clicks and keystrokes)
  • Shorter time spent finishing the task
  • Subjective Question: The user "feels" an interface to be more effective, by asking:
    • On a scale of 5, with 1 being extremely bad and 5 being extremely good, how many points would you give to Interface A vs. Interface B in terms of how good the interface was?

The data of interest will depend on what usability goal or design principle. Choose and test at least one of the usability goals or design principles. This specific goal or principle should be the one from your project description, unless noted otherwise. Send me an email (song100@usf.edu) if none of the questions below apply to your project.

The following describes the required information to gather for each goal/principle:

 Efficiency

  • Indicators
    • Fewer inputs or shorter time spent, as noted above, are the core elements of efficiency. Compare them.
    • Amount of outcome (e.g., amount of information, information presentation, or information accuracy) can be optionally compared if the amount of inputs or time spent is not different between your prototype and the existing solution.
  • Subjective question: Ask the following questions after the user finishes the task in both the prototype and the existing solution:
    • With 1 being extremely challenging to 5 being extremely effortless, how many points would you give to Interface A vs. Interface B?
    • (after the participant answers the question above) What was the reason that made you give those points to each of them?

 Safety

  • Indicators
    • Count the number of errors that the user made during the task. A smaller number of errors presents a safer user interface.
    • Account for the importance of the error in your analysis. If the error is easily fixable, making an error does not really matter. If the error is critical, then more errors mean a less safe interface. The importance of the mistake is up to your judgment, but a sound reason should be provided.
  • Subjective question: Ask the following questions after the user finishes the task in both the prototype and the existing solution:
    • While using Interface A and Interface B, which one would you say protects the user better?
    • On a scale of 5, with 1 being extremely low and 5 being extremely high, how many points would you give to Interface A vs. Interface B in terms of how much they protect the users?
    • Why do you think A (or B) better protects the user?

 Utility

  • Indicators
    • Count the number of choices that users can make during each step of the task. Combine the numbers for your prototype and for the design alternative, separately.
    • If the goal that the user achieved is the same (likely the case), fewer choice is an indicator of a more specified (i.e., better utility)
  • Subjective question: Ask the following questions after the user finishes the task in both the prototype and the existing solution:
    • On a scale of 5, with 1 being extremely low and 5 being extremely high, how many points would you give to Interface A vs. Interface B in terms of how useful they are in finishing the task that you were given?
    • Why do you think A (or B) was more useful?

 Learnability or Memorability

  • Indicators
    Assess and compare the accuracy and the amount of detail from your participants' answers. Provide a transcript for this part. To do so, conduct a memory test by asking the following questions after the user finishes the task in both the prototype and the existing solution:
    • Please recite the steps, from the beginning to the end, that you had to follow to complete the task in interface A.
    • Please recite the steps, from the beginning to the end, that you had to follow to complete the task in the interface B.
  • Subjective question: Ask the following questions after the user finishes the task in both the prototype and the existing solution:
    • On a scale of 5, with 1 being extremely challenging and 5 being extremely effortless, how many points would you give to Interface A vs. Interface B in terms of how easy it was to learn and remember how to finish the task?
    • Why do you think A (or B) was more useful?

 Visibility

  • Indicators
    • Count the number of errors that the user made during the task. A smaller number of errors presents a safer user interface.
    • Account for the importance of the error in your analysis. If the error is easily fixable, making an error does not really matter. If the error is critical, then more errors mean a less safe interface. The importance of the mistake is up to your judgment, but a sound reason should be provided.
    • There should be a core interface element that you are testing for visibility. After users finish their task on both Interfaces A and B, ask users if they were actually visible through the following question:
      • Please describe Button X in detail, to your best ability.
  • Subjective question: Ask the following questions after the user finishes the task in both the prototype and the existing solution:
    • On a scale of 5, with 1 being extremely challenging and 5 being extremely effortless, how many points would you give to Interface A vs. Interface B in terms of how easy it was to find button X?
    • Why do you think A (or B) was more useful?

 Feedback

  • Feedback should not be your only goal/principle. Choose and test another goal/principle that best aligns with your idea. That other one should be one of the following: Efficiency, Safety, Utility, Learnability/Memorability.
  • To measure the efficacy of the feedback that you added to the interface, check it by asking the following question after the user finishes tasks on both Interface A and Interface B:
    • What did you notice from the interface after you pressed button X?

 Constraints

  • Indicators
    • Count the number of errors that the user made during the task. A smaller number of errors presents a safer user interface.
    • Account for the importance of the error in your analysis. If the error is easily fixable, making an error does not really matter. If the error is critical, then more errors mean a less safe interface. The importance of the mistake is up to your judgment, but a sound reason should be provided.
  • Subjective question: Ask the following questions after the user finishes the task in both the prototype and the existing solution:
    • While using Interface A and Interface B, which one would you say protects the user better?
    • On a scale of 5, with 1 being extremely low and 5 being extremely high, how many points would you give to Interface A vs. Interface B in terms of how much they protect the users?
    • Why do you think A (or B) better protects the user?

 Consistency

Consistency is a given these days when it comes to interface designs. Consistency should not be your only or primary goal/principle. Choose and test another goal/principle that best aligns with your idea. That other one should be one of the following: Efficiency, Safety, Utility, Learnability/Memorability.

 Affordance

  • Affordance should not be your only goal/principle. Choose and test another goal/principle that best aligns with your idea. That other one should be one of the following: Efficiency, Safety, Utility, Learnability/Memorability.
  • To check the affordance, ask the following question (Button X can be swapped into any other important interface element to which you applied affordance; Interface A here stands for your prototype):
    • Please describe Button X from Interface A in as much detail as possible.
    • What did Button X remind you of as you laid your eyes on them?
    • In what way did Button X contribute to a better experience from Interface A compared to Interface B? Why was it that way?
